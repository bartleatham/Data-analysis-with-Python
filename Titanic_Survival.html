1- Did the Socio-economic status (SES) affect the survival rate?
We will assume that the passenger class (Pclass) is representative of the SES of the passenger:
Pclass 1 -> high SES
Pclass 2 -> mid SES
Pclass 3 -> low SES

survivors_by_class = df.groupby('Pclass')['Survived'].mean()

The First Class Survival Rate was 62.96%.
The Second Class Survival Rate was 47.28%.
The Third Class Survival Rate was 24.24%.

It is clear from the calculated survival rate percentages, and the graphical visualization that the passenger class, and thus the assumed SES did have a significant impact on the chances of survival, with those in higher status having a greater chance of survival. From the data it is unclear how the SES impact is manifested. It is safe to assume that the higher the passenger class, the closer the cabins are to the upper decks, and thus to the lifeboats. It is possible that a passenger of higher SES, even if located in a lower passenger class cabin could have been given preferential treatment in evacuation by their fellow passengers. Again, we do not have the proper data to substantiate these hypothesis.

Conclusion:¶

Due to the limited and incomplete data set provided for this analysis, and without performing statistical analysis on the given sample, the answers to our data exploration questions cannot be determined with any degree of confidence, nor can we claim that any of the correlations we found to be the cause or survival.
The data set provided was a sample of the population of passengers on board the Titanic. We have no idea what the demographics of the missing passengers were, they could have all been children that died, or men that lived. Both of these hypothetical cases would have changed our results significantly.
There was missing age demographical data in the data set provided. I chose to remove the passengers with missing age data from any analysis that used age as one of the independant variables. By remove these passenger records, we are ignoring close to 20% of the sample data. This severly limits the accuracy of our conclusions. Another solution to dealing with the passenger records with missing age demographics would have been to fill in the missing data with values, such as the mean or median age of our sample. This solution would still have severly limited the accuracy of our conclusions.
Given the above limitations, and recognizing that we are not performing any statistical analysis in this study, it is my hope that the reader should feel that in the case of the Titanic Tragedy, and out of respect for the many that perished, that chivalry was not dead.
